The basics

What is a Digital Product Passport?

A DPP is a structured set of data about a product - where it was made, what's in it, how to care for it, and what to do with it at end of life. Encoded in a QR code on the label.

🏷️

A QR code on the label

Scan it and reach a structured data record - not a PDF, not a marketing page. Machine-readable information rendered automatically.

📋

Regulated data fields

The EU specifies what information must be included - fibre composition, recycled content, supply chain, repairability, end-of-life guidance. Some fields are public; others are restricted to specific actors.

🔗

Lives with the product

A DPP follows the garment through its life - raw material, manufacture, retail, resale, repair, recycling.

⚖️

Required by ESPR

The Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (Reg. EU 2024/1781) is the legal basis. A delegated act specifying exact requirements is expected in 2026.

🏭

The brand is responsible

The manufacturer - which in fashion typically means the brand that markets the product - is legally responsible for the information in the DPP.

🌍

Applies to imports too

Any product placed on the EU market needs a DPP, including products made outside the EU. The importer or EU authorised representative takes on the manufacturer's obligations.

Why open-dpp exists

Navigating the standards landscape

01

Too many bodies, too little signal

GS1, UNTP, CEN/CENELEC JTC 24, ISO, CIRPASS - all active, all producing documents, none yet mandated. The EU hasn't specified which standards DPPs must conform to. Brands are being asked to prepare for compliance without a clear target.

02

Standards documents are not implementation guides

A 120-page PDF describing a data model is not the same thing as knowing what to put in a database. open-dpp makes this clear so you don't have to start from scratch.

03

The delegated act is still pending

ESPR gives the Commission power to specify exact requirements via delegated acts. The textile act hasn't been adopted yet. This means some requirements are confirmed and not.

04

Openness is the point

DPP infrastructure built on proprietary platforms creates lock-in and fragmentation. A shared open reference schema is a public good. Fork it, adapt it, contribute back.

"

The QR code on a garment should tell you what's in it, where it was made, how to care for it, and what to do with it when you're done. That's it. The rest is implementation detail.
